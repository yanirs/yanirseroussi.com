Dataland Venns

  • Early on, you need generalists

Ask de-hyping questions

Plumbing:
What's the state of your data engineering lifecycles?
Decisions:
How do you use descriptive, predictive, and causal modelling to support decisions?
Automation:
How do you use AI to automate processes?
  • Data engineering lifecycle: Generation, storage, ingestion, transformation, serving
  • Descriptive: Analytics, dashboards, one-off reports
  • Predictive: Machine learning, statistics, using maths to learn patterns from data and predict/forecast unknowns
  • Causal: Experimentation (e.g., A/B testing) or more sophisticated approaches when controlled experiments are impossible
  • AI: Think of it as automation – can be user-facing or internal (was always a marketing term for automation)

Principles before tools

  • If you start with tools, you'll drive yourself MAD
  • Work from first principles and business problems that are important now

Some useful terms

Basic AI:
Black boxes like ChatGPT and transcription tools
Advanced AI/ML:
Custom models, harder, requires MLOps & serious data work beyond the prototype
Analytics:
The M in Build-Measure-Learn, key to product-led growth – depends on plumbing
  • Data is at the root of advanced AI/ML: you're unlikely to innovate on algorithms
  • Data is like solar energy: most of it goes uncaptured – you can turn it into business value
  • Above all, avoid getting too excited – advanced AI/ML and solid analytics are hard (keep your short-term dreams realistic)

Before
hiring

What do you need from Dataland?

  • Is it important for near-term growth?
  • List responsibilities, not titles
  • Make a 90-day plan
  • Define a successful outcome
  • Ensure everyone agrees
  • If you can't articulate a 90-day plan, how will you know they're successful?
  • If there is no work for 90 days, you shouldn't hire a full-timer
  • No one is coming to save you: Don't expect an employee to define their role to the benefit of the company
  • My experience: "tell us what to do with data" – yeah nah, you know your customers better than I do (at least early on)

Consider not hiring

  • Do urgent work in-house?
  • Get a contractor / fractional?
  • Defer non-urgent work?

Achieving outcomes
without hiring
=
Massive win

  • In-house by people with adjacent skills may be good enough as a start (e.g., software eng setting up data pipelines)
  • Not just saying to consider fractionals due to what I do, as simply not doing non-urgent work is also an option
  • Why win? Deferred an expensive decision, got what you needed, and gained better understanding of future needs
  • Maybe highlight my experience: Premature hiring because there's money or due to future needs; remember the opportunity cost!

Deciding
to
hire

Get even clearer on the role

  • 30 & 90 day plans
  • Aspirational goals for 6-12 month horizon
  • Likely level: mid-senior
  • Possible titles:
    • Data tech lead (analytics focus)
    • AI/ML lead (engineering focus)
  • Compensation isn't just salary + equity
  • Again, if you have no idea about first 90 days, don't hire
  • Anything beyond a few months is aspirational for startups, but it matters since you're hiring a permanent role
  • Mid-senior: Can lead a team, hands-on, able to make tech decisions and speak truth to power
  • Too junior: Waste of money
  • Too senior: Unlikely to be hands-on, expensive, not hungry
  • Seniority is correlated with years of experience, but not perfectly; curiosity and growth mindset are key (20 YoE – same year twenty times?)
  • Beyond salary and equity: 80% or 90% time; learning & growth; the mission matters (ensure you highlight it)

Get help

  • (Good) recruiters are not the enemy
  • External recruiters: Help with sourcing and understanding the market
  • Internal recruiters: Help with the process and screening
  • Specialists: Help with technical screening
  • Remember the principal-agent problem
  • Barrier of entry to recruitment is low, so a lot of bad ones exist
  • Paying $5-30k to increase probability of success with a $100-200k investment should be a no-brainer
  • Tech screening applies to hiring outside your expertise in general; get the right person (e.g., analytics / ML specialist)
  • Principal-agent: Having someone on your side with aligned interests is ideal (internal), but out of reach early on; ensure that interests align to the best of your ability

The hiring process

Run a process you'd love

  • Be clear and honest
  • Be responsive and efficient
  • Ask relevant, informative questions
  • Remember you're not Google
  • Honesty about where you're at helps you stand out; also helps with retention (don't bullshit candidates)
  • Clarity includes clarity on the process and needs
  • Responsive and efficient: A quick process is a competitive advantage, and you want to get back to shipping ASAP
  • Relevant and informative: Derive from responsibilities and 90-day plan; check industry best practices; remember Chatty
  • Not Google is an advantage: Move fast with less bullshit; remember that people talk and your reputation can be tarnished

Sample process

  1. Ad: Clear & honest; include process, salary range, 30-90 day plans, and 6-12 month aspirations
  2. Simple call to action with custom questions
  3. Initial screen options:
    • Reject quickly and with note; or
    • Invite to 30-minute intro call
  4. Technical screen
  5. Final screen & offer
  • Custom questions: Avoid cover letter ChatGPT bullshit; relevant to your screening decisions
  • Initial screen: Intro call is a low-pressure two-way street; understand candidate expectations and see if there's high-level alignment
  • Technical screen in next slide
  • Final screen may include culture fit and reference checks

Relevant, informative questions

  • Derive from responsibilities and 90-day plan
  • Dig into past work and current motivations
  • Get techies to explain to non-tech staff
  • Technical screen:
    • Simple live screen questions
    • Take-home task
    • Dig into take-home response in call
    • Consider paid trials & contract-to-hire
  • Ask each question once
  • Only ask questions that add information (hence the responsibilities and plan)
  • Past work: Deep on what they've done and why; current motivations especially relevant around why they want the role
  • Clarity of non-tech communication is especially important for first data hire (interfaces with rest of company)
  • Simple questions can be tested by non-techies (Google recruiter screen as example)
  • Take-home task should be time-boxed like CBA to reduce chance of cheating
  • Digging live into take-home work ensures they understand what they did (especially important with Chatty)
  • Paid trials & contract-to-hire: Depends on candidates; possible with a large qualified pool

Above all...
Keep it
efficient &
respectful

  • Respect your candidates – career arcs are long
  • Don't forget they may interview you one day
  • If you run a crappy process, good candidates will run away

Avoid common pitfalls

  • Ignoring experts
  • Trusting the wrong experts
  • Google worship
  • Hunting unicorns
  • Expecting magic
  • Hiring narrow specialists
  • Ignoring experts: Recruiters speak to thousands of candidates – often-ignored expertise
  • Trusting the wrong experts: I'm not a recruitment expert; haven't done it enough – take what I say with a grain of salt
  • Google (or other BigName) worship: Pedigree is often irrelevant to startups (Google hires plenty of duds), Google processes aren't fit for purpose, Googlers may not work well outside of a big org
  • Unicorns: Hard to find and retain; focus on what you need to get done
  • Magic: Data/AI/ML is hard, recruiting perfect humans is impossible – gotta compromise within reason
  • Narrow specialists: Aren't a good fit for a startup

Recap: Key takeaways

  • Data & AI: Plumbing, decisions, and automation
  • Before hiring, get clear on your needs
  • Outcomes first: Consider alternatives to hiring
  • If hiring, run a process you'd love
  • Keep learning, get help, and avoid pitfalls
